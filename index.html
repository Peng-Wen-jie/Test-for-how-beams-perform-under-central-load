Abstract

Investigation of how beams made by different materials with various geometry features perform under central load

July 30, 2020 Peng Wenjie

This project concerns the research and implementations of Young’s modulus of elasticity, relationships between curvature, rotation and deflection and introduce the basic concepts of structural and material engineering through a beam loading test. In the experiment, we use weights to apply a load at the centre of the beam and measure the resulting mid-span deflection. After finding the Young’s Modulus value and calculated the second moment of inertia value for beam of particular material and cross-section geometry, I calculated the theoretical central deflection value for each beam under ascending load. This experiment provides data for discussing material engineering topics, such as elastic and inelastic behaviour, the influence of materials and structural shapes, stiffness and strength.

Introduction

Young’s modulus of elasticity plays an important role in engineering and material. It is a characteristic of the material which is not dependent on the stress or on the relative deformation. There are several ways to determine the modulus of elasticity—mechanical, acoustics, ultrasound, resonance and optics. Mechanical methods are best used to determine the elastic modulus of thin materials such as rods, wires, and fibres. Ohtsuki defined a new method for measuring Young's modulus of flexible materials based on the large bending strain of a sample subjected to axial compression at two fixed ends. This method eliminates unnecessary loading nose effects common in three-point or four-point flexion. Two types of flexible materials (PVC: high polymer content sheet, SWPA: piano wire) have been tested, and the results show that the method is suitable for flexible materials, such as long thin fibre materials (fibreglass, carbon fibre, optical fibre, etc.) or thin sheets material. Mirambelland Real studied the bending behaviour of stainless steel beams and calculated the maximum deflection under different load levels (especially under service conditions).
